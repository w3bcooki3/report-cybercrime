Your Guide to Phishing Awareness

Learn how to recognize, report, and defend against phishing attacks with our comprehensive guide - from beginners to experts.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where criminals attempt to trick you into revealing sensitive information or installing malicious software. They typically pose as trustworthy entities like your bank, email provider, or a company you do business with.

Did you know? Phishing attacks account for more than 80% of reported security incidents and cost businesses billions of dollars annually.
📧

Email Phishing

Deceptive emails that appear to come from legitimate organizations, asking you to verify information or click on suspicious links.

📱

Smishing

SMS phishing that uses text messages to trick you into downloading malware or visiting malicious websites.

🔊

Vishing

Voice phishing where scammers call pretending to be from tech support, banks, or government agencies to steal your information.

🎣

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks against specific individuals or organizations using personalized information to appear more credible.

How to Recognize Phishing Attempts

The first step in protecting yourself is learning to identify suspicious messages. Here are the key warning signs to look for:

Phishing Example

From: secure-banking@bankofamerica-secure.com

Subject: URGENT: Your Account Has Been Compromised

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your account has been temporarily limited. Please verify your information immediately by clicking the link below to avoid permanent account suspension.

https://www.bankofamerica-secure-verification.com/login

If you don't verify within 24 hours, your account will be permanently suspended.

Bank of America Security Team

Common Red Flags:

  • Urgency and threats - Messages creating panic or threatening negative consequences
  • Grammar and spelling errors - Professional organizations typically proofread their communications
  • Suspicious sender email address - Look carefully at the domain (what comes after @)
  • Suspicious links - Hover over links without clicking to see the actual URL destination
  • Requests for sensitive information - Legitimate organizations rarely ask for passwords or full account numbers via email
  • Too good to be true offers - Unexpected prizes, inheritance, or deals that seem unrealistic
  • Generic greetings - "Dear Customer" instead of your name

Pro Tip: Check the URL

Always verify the website address before entering sensitive information. Phishing sites often use domains that look similar to legitimate ones but have subtle differences:

Legitimate: https://www.bankofamerica.com

Phishing: https://www.bankofamerica-secure.com

Notice the additional words or hyphens in the phishing URL.

Email Header Analysis

For more technical users, examining email headers can reveal valuable information:

Legitimate Email Headers

• Sender and "Reply-To" addresses match

• SPF, DKIM, and DMARC pass security checks

• IP addresses match the claimed organization

• Consistent return path

Suspicious Email Headers

• Mismatched sender and "Reply-To" addresses

• Failed SPF, DKIM, or DMARC checks

• IP addresses from unexpected countries

• Routing through multiple unusual servers

Remember: Phishing attempts get more sophisticated every day. If you're unsure about a message, it's always better to verify through official channels (like calling the company directly using a number from their official website - not from the suspicious email).

How to Protect Yourself from Phishing

Essential Security Practices

  • Use strong, unique passwords for each of your important accounts
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) whenever possible
  • Keep software and devices updated with the latest security patches
  • Use security software including antivirus and anti-malware protection
  • Be cautious with unexpected attachments and only download from trusted sources
  • Verify website security by looking for HTTPS and the padlock icon in your address bar

Password Security Best Practices

Create Strong Passwords

• At least 12 characters long

• Mix of uppercase and lowercase letters

• Include numbers and special characters

• Avoid personal information or common words

Use a Password Manager

• Generates unique, complex passwords

• Securely stores your credentials

• Auto-fills forms on legitimate sites

• Helps avoid password reuse

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

• Requires something you know (password)

• Plus something you have (phone, security key)

• Provides protection even if password is compromised

• Often uses authenticator apps or SMS codes

Pro Tip: Consider using a passphrase (a sequence of random words) instead of a traditional password. For example, "correct-horse-battery-staple" is both strong and easier to remember than "P@s$w0rd123!".

How to Report Phishing Attempts

Reporting phishing attempts helps protect others and improves security systems. Here's how to report different types of phishing:

Steps to Report Phishing Emails

  1. Forward the email to your organization's IT security team or help desk
  2. Report to the impersonated organization (e.g., forward to reportphishing@amazon.comMost major companies have dedicated email addresses for reporting phishing attempts. if it's claiming to be from Amazon)
  3. Report to your email provider (most have a "Report Phishing" option)
  4. Report to government agencies:
    • US: Forward to phishing@irs.gov and report@ic3.gov (FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center)
    • UK: Forward to report@phishing.gov.uk
    • Canada: Report to the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre
    • Australia: Report to ScamWatch

Defending Against Advanced Phishing Tactics

As phishing techniques evolve, so should your defense strategies. Here are ways to protect against sophisticated attacks:

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

BEC attacks target businesses through spoofed executive emails, often requesting wire transfers or sensitive information.

Warning: BEC attacks have cost businesses over $26 billion worldwide according to the FBI.

Prevention Strategies

• Implement DMARC, SPF, and DKIM email authentication

• Establish verification procedures for financial requests

• Create notification systems for executive travel

• Train employees to recognize impersonation attempts

Warning Signs

• Requests that bypass normal procedures

• Emails emphasizing secrecy or urgency

• Slightly altered email domains (CEO@company-inc.com vs CEO@company.com)

• Writing style different from the executive's typical communications

Clone Phishing

Clone phishing replicates legitimate emails previously sent but replaces attachments or links with malicious versions.

Important: If you receive a duplicate email with attachments or links, verify with the sender before interacting with the content, even if you've received similar emails before.

Pharming Attacks

Pharming poisoning redirects traffic from legitimate websites to fake ones, even when you type the correct URL.

DNS Poisoning Protection

• Use trusted DNS providers

• Enable DNSSEC validation

• Keep routers and devices updated

• Use VPN services on public networks

Always Verify Connection Security

• Look for HTTPS and valid certificates

• Check for certificate errors

• Verify site identity information

• Be alert for unusual site behavior

Phishing Simulation

Interactive Email Analysis

Practice identifying phishing emails with this interactive example. Analyze the email below and decide what action to take.

From: microsoft-noreply@microsoft-securityteam.com

To: you@company.com

Subject: IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED: Security Alert on Your Microsoft Account

Date: Today, 10:45 AM

Dear Microsoft Customer,

We have detected unusual sign-in activity on your Microsoft account from an unrecognized device. To ensure your account security, we have temporarily limited access to sensitive account features.

Please verify your identity immediately by logging in to your account using the secure link below:

https://microsoft-secure-accountverify.com/signin

If you don't complete verification within 24 hours, your account may be suspended for security reasons.

Microsoft Account Protection Team

Correct! This is a phishing email. Red flags include:
  • The sender email domain "microsoft-securityteam.com" is not a legitimate Microsoft domain
  • The urgent tone creating a sense of fear
  • The link points to a suspicious URL, not to microsoft.com
  • The threat of account suspension to pressure you into acting quickly
