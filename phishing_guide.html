1Email Phishing

The most common form of phishing where attackers send fraudulent emails pretending to be from legitimate organizations.

Red Flags:

  • Urgent or threatening language
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Mismatched or suspicious email domains
  • Requests for personal information
  • Unexpected attachments

2Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks that use personal information to create highly customized, convincing messages aimed at specific individuals.

Red Flags:

  • Uses your personal details (name, job title, etc.)
  • References your company or colleagues
  • Appears to come from a trusted source
  • Creates a compelling reason to act quickly

3Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attempts via text messages that often contain malicious links or request sensitive information.

Red Flags:

  • Unexpected texts from banks or services
  • Shortened links (bit.ly, tinyurl, etc.)
  • Messages creating urgency or fear
  • Requests to call unfamiliar numbers

4Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone-based phishing scams where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal information or money.

Red Flags:

  • Caller creates urgency or threats
  • Requests for verification of personal details
  • Callers from "tech support" or government agencies
  • Use of robocalls with suspicious call-to-action

5Pharming

Redirects users from legitimate websites to fraudulent ones by exploiting vulnerabilities in DNS servers or the user's computer.

Red Flags:

  • You're redirected to an unexpected website
  • The URL doesn't match the expected domain
  • Website looks different from usual
  • Missing HTTPS or security indicators

6Clone Phishing

Attackers create nearly identical copies of legitimate messages but replace links or attachments with malicious ones.

Red Flags:

  • Looks very similar to previous legitimate communications
  • Claims to be an updated or resent version
  • Subtle differences in sender address
  • Links that don't match the stated destination

7Whaling

Targeted phishing attacks specifically aimed at senior executives or high-profile individuals in an organization.

Red Flags:

  • Highly personalized content regarding executive matters
  • Mimics communication styles of other executives
  • Often involves financial requests or sensitive information
  • May reference board meetings or confidential topics

8Business Email Compromise

Sophisticated scams targeting businesses, often involving compromise of legitimate email accounts to conduct unauthorized transfers or data theft.

Red Flags:

  • Sudden changes in wire transfer instructions
  • Requests to change vendor payment information
  • Pressure for secrecy or urgency
  • Emails from executives with slight variations in domain

9Social Media Phishing

Phishing attempts through social media platforms using fake profiles, malicious links, or fraudulent apps.

Red Flags:

  • Friend/connection requests from unknown accounts
  • Messages with suspicious links or attachments
  • Fake giveaways or contest notifications
  • Requests to login to view content

10Search Engine Phishing

Creating fake websites that appear in search results to trick users into visiting malicious sites that steal information.

Red Flags:

  • Too-good-to-be-true deals on popular products
  • Sponsored results with slight typos in domain names
  • Sites lacking proper security certificates
  • Poor website design or functionality

11QR Code Phishing

Using malicious QR codes that direct users to fraudulent websites designed to steal credentials or install malware.

Red Flags:

  • QR codes in unexpected locations or contexts
  • Codes that redirect to suspicious URLs
  • Stickers placed over legitimate QR codes
  • QR codes with urgent calls to action

12Man-in-the-Middle Attacks

Attackers intercept communications between two parties to steal data or credentials, often via unsecured Wi-Fi.

Red Flags:

  • Unsecured public Wi-Fi connections
  • Certificate warnings in browsers
  • Unexpected disconnections during browsing
  • Being asked to re-authenticate on a known website

How to Protect Yourself

General Protection Measures:

  • Keep your devices and software updated
  • Use strong, unique passwords for each account
  • Enable two-factor authentication whenever possible
  • Be skeptical of unexpected communications
  • Verify requests through official channels
  • Don't click on suspicious links or open unexpected attachments
