What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack often used to steal user data, including login credentials, credit card numbers, and other sensitive information. It occurs when an attacker, masquerading as a trusted entity, dupes a victim into opening an email, instant message, or text message.

Did you know?
Phishing attacks account for more than 80% of reported security incidents and cost businesses billions of dollars annually.

Types of Phishing

What started as crude email scams has evolved into highly targeted attacks that use advanced social engineering tactics. Today's phishing attempts are often indistinguishable from legitimate communications.

Common Types of Phishing Attacks

Email Phishing

The most common form of phishing, where attackers send fraudulent emails purporting to be from reputable companies to steal personal information or install malware.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attempts sent via text messages that often contain malicious links or ask for sensitive information while impersonating trusted organizations.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone-based phishing where attackers use social engineering over the phone to gain access to private information or funds, often by creating a sense of urgency.

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks directed at specific individuals or companies, using personalized information to appear more legitimate and convincing.

Whaling

A form of spear phishing that specifically targets senior executives and other high-profile targets within businesses, often with the goal of financial gain.

Clone Phishing

Attacks where legitimate and previously delivered emails are used to create malicious copies, replacing links or attachments with malicious versions.

Red Flags to Watch For

Suspicious Sender Address

Look closely at the sender's email address. Phishing emails often use domains that mimic legitimate ones with slight variations (e.g., amazon-support.com instead of amazon.com).

Poor Grammar and Spelling

Professional organizations typically proofread their communications. Multiple spelling and grammatical errors are a common indicator of phishing attempts.

Urgent or Threatening Language

Be wary of messages creating a sense of urgency, threatening negative consequences, or offering rewards that seem too good to be true.

Suspicious Links and Attachments

Hover over links (don't click) to see where they actually lead. Be cautious of unexpected attachments, especially executable files (.exe, .scr, etc.).

Requests for Sensitive Information

Legitimate organizations rarely request sensitive personal information via email. Be suspicious of any message asking for passwords, credit card numbers, or Social Security numbers.

Pro Tip: When in doubt, don't click or respond. Instead, contact the purported sender directly through their official website or phone number (not the one provided in the suspicious message).

How to Protect Yourself

Use Strong Authentication

Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) whenever possible. Use strong, unique passwords for each account and consider using a password manager.

Verify Before You Trust

Always verify the source of emails, especially those requesting sensitive information or urgent action. Use official channels to confirm suspicious communications.

Stay Informed

Keep up to date with the latest phishing techniques and scams. Regular security awareness training can significantly reduce your vulnerability.

Technical Safeguards

Keep Software Updated

Regularly update your operating system, browsers, and security software to protect against known vulnerabilities.

Use Email Filtering

Implement robust spam filters that can detect viruses, blank senders, and known phishing attempt characteristics.

Install Browser Extensions

Consider security-focused browser extensions that can help identify and block malicious websites.

Secure Your Network

Use a firewall, encrypt your wireless network, and consider using a VPN for additional security, especially on public networks.

Remember: No security measure is perfect on its own. Layering multiple protections and maintaining vigilance is your best defense against phishing attacks.

Phishing Attack Trends

Industry Insight: Phishing attempts spike during major global events, holidays, and tax seasons. Be especially vigilant during these periods.
