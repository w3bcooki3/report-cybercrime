Introduction

Cybercrime is a growing concern in today's digital world. Threats such as phishing, ransomware, and identity theft can cause significant financial and personal damage. This guide will help both tech-savvy and non-technical individuals recognize different types of cyber threats and how to prevent them.

Cyber incidents can happen to anyone, regardless of technical expertise. Whether you're a professional working online or someone who uses the internet casually, here is a full detailed guide on how to protect yourself from cyber threats.

1. Use Strong and Secure Passwords

  • Enable strong, unique passwords for all your online accounts (Use a combination of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Avoid easily guessable information like birthdays or names.), and consider using a reputable password manager to keep track of them.
    Below are the a. w to create a strong password:

    a. Start with a Passphrase or Random Words:
    Choose a passphrase or a combination of random words that are meaningful to you but not easily guessable. For example, "BlueSky$ChocolateRain" or "MountainBike@SunnyTrail."

    b. Mix Uppercase and Lowercase Letters:
    Mix uppercase and lowercase letters in your passphrase or word combination. Alternate between them to add complexity. For example, "BlUeSkY$cHoCoLaTeRaIn" or "MoUnTaInBiKe@SuNnYtRaIl."

    c. Add Numbers:
    Insert numbers within your passphrase, preferably in a non-sequential manner. You can replace certain letters with numbers that resemble them. For example, "BlU3SkY$cHoC0LaTeR@1n" or "M0UnTa1nB1k3@SuNnY7Ra1l."

    d. Include Special Characters:
    Integrate special characters like !, @, #, $, %, etc., into your password. Place them strategically within your passphrase. For example, "BlU3SkY$cHoC0!@TeR@1n" or "M0UnTa1nB1k3@SuNnY7#Ra1l."

    e. Length Matters:
    Aim for a password length of at least 12 characters. The longer your password, the stronger it is against hacking attempts.

    f. Don't Use Predictable Patterns:
    Avoid using predictable patterns, such as "123456" or "qwerty," as they are easily cracked.

    g. Personal Touch:
    Add a personal touch or abbreviation to the passphrase that only you would understand. For example, "BlU3SkY$cHoC0!@TeR@1n_MyDog" or "M0UnTa1nB1k3@SuNnY7#Ra1l_HiSchool."

    h. Test Its Strength:
    Before finalizing your password, use online password strength checkers to ensure it meets the desired level of complexity.

  • Keep It Private. Never share your password with anyone. Treat it as a sensitive piece of information.
  • Change passwords regularly. Consider changing your password every 30-60 days to enhance security
  • Use Password Managers. Consider using a reputable password manager to generate, store, and autofill your strong passwords securely.
    For example: Dashlane, Lastpass are good Password Manager

2. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

  • MFA adds an extra layer of security by requiring a second form of verification (e.g., a text message code or authentication app) along with your password. It is recommended to activate MFA on your email, banking apps, social media, and any platform that offers it.
  • Set up two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever possible to add an extra layer of security to your accounts.
    To see a list of 2-factor authentication softwares, visit our Cyber Toolkit page.

3. Recognize and Avoid Phishing Scams

Phishing scams trick people into giving away sensitive information.

  • Check the Sender:
    Always verify email addresses before clicking on links or downloading attachments.
  • Look for Red Flags:
    Generic greetings, spelling errors, urgent requests, and suspicious links are signs of phishing.
  • Do Not Click Unknown Links:
    Avoid clicking on suspicious links or downloading attachments from unknown sources, especially in emails or messages.Instead, hover over links to preview their true destination.
  • Report Suspicious Emails:
    Notify your email provider or company's IT department if you receive a phishing attempt.
  • Be Cautious of Online Activites:
    Be wary of sharing personal information online, especially on social media platforms. Cybercriminals can exploit this information for phishing attacks. Limit What You Share on Social Media.

4. Keep Your Devices and Software Updated

  • Enable Automatic Updates:
    Regular update softwares and applications on your device(s)
  • Update Your Antivirus Software:
    Keep security programs updated to detect and block the latest threats.

5. Secure Your Internet Connection

  • Use Strong Wi-Fi Passwords:
    Prevent unauthorized access to your home network.
  • Avoid Public Wi-Fi for Sensitive Transactions:
    Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) when connecting to public hotspots.
  • Turn Off Bluetooth & Wi-Fi When Not in Use:
    This reduces exposure to cyber attacks.

6. Secure Your Online Transactions

  • Enable Alerts for Bank Transactions:
    Monitor transactions in real time to detect fraud early.

7. Protect Your Social Media Accounts

  • Use Unique Passwords for Each Account:
    Prevent hackers from accessing multiple accounts if one is compromised.
  • Enable Login Notifications:
    Receive alerts when there is a new login attempt.
  • Review Connected Apps & Devices:
    Remove unnecessary third-party applications that have access to your accounts.
  • Enable MFA (2FA) Authentication:
    Activate two-factor authentication to prevent access to your accounts even if hackers know your password.

8. Report Cyber Incidents Immediately

If you experience a cyber attack or suspect fraudulent activity, take these steps:

  • Report to Authorities:
    Contact your country's cybercrime reporting agency.
  • Notify Your Bank or Service Provider:
    Block transactions or accounts if financial details were exposed.
  • Inform Your IT Department or Employer:
    If you're working for a company, report incidents to IT security teams.
  • Change Compromised Passwords:
    Secure affected accounts to prevent further damage.
